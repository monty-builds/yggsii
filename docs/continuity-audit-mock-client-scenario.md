# Continuity Audit Mock Client Scenario

This note pressure-tests the continuity-audit offer against a plausible early client.

The goal is to see whether the current offer, intake path, and sample output feel concrete enough to survive contact with an actual messy project.

## Mock client

Name: Rowan

Project type: ongoing tabletop campaign being reshaped into a prose serial

Current state:
- 18 sessions worth of material
- rough recap notes rather than polished chapters
- ensemble cast with six recurring characters
- two major locations, then a travel arc with several temporary stops
- hidden agenda around one character and an unreliable account of a failed mission

## What Rowan says hurts

- I keep losing track of who knew what, when.
- I think the travel timeline is fuzzy after session 7.
- One character vanished for a while and I am not sure whether it reads as intentional or accidental.
- The rival faction keeps feeling important in theory and muddy in practice.
- I do not want line editing. I want someone to tell me where the structure is lying to me.

## Why this is a good test case

This is close to the ideal first buyer profile.

The continuity pain is obvious.

The material is structured enough to audit, but messy enough that the value proposition should feel tangible.

It also tests whether the offer language works outside straight novel framing.

## Would the current intake checklist handle this

Mostly yes.

Why:
- the project has a recoverable scene or session order
- the client can likely provide cast and location lists
- the main worries fit the continuity lane directly
- the project sits under the current suggested cap if the first pass is limited to one arc

What would need tightening:
- the checklist should explicitly mention session-based material, not just scenes and chapters
- the intake may need a field for knowledge-state problems, because "who knows what when" is a recurring continuity failure mode
- the offer should make clear that recap notes are acceptable input if they are ordered

## Would the current offer sheet feel persuasive

Mostly yes, but with two gaps.

### Gap 1, campaign language should be more explicit

The current offer sheet mentions tabletop campaigns, which is good.

But the mock client's phrasing is stronger than the current copy. "I want someone to tell me where the structure is lying to me" is nearly the pitch.

That suggests the current positioning is directionally right, but could use one sharper line aimed at campaign and adaptation mess.

### Gap 2, hidden knowledge should be named directly

The current materials mention continuity, chronology, and relationships, but not knowledge-state logic.

For many ensemble stories, especially campaigns and fanfiction, continuity failure is often epistemic rather than spatial.

That means the offer likely gets stronger if it explicitly names:
- who knows what, when
- secret reveals
- canon or lore constraints

## What the sample audit proves here

The sample audit already proves:
- the memo can sound useful without being overblown
- the audit can identify pressure-drop risk, missing foreshadowing, and weak handoffs
- the service feels more like structural diagnosis than generic commentary

What it does not yet prove:
- handling of larger casts than the Yggsii demo
- hidden information logic
- branchy or adaptation-shaped material

## Result of the pressure test

The offer survives.

It feels plausible for this client.

The weak point is not the core promise. The weak point is that the wording still leans slightly novel-centric, and it undernames knowledge-state continuity.

## Concrete improvements suggested by this test

1. Update the intake checklist to mention ordered session recaps as acceptable source material.
2. Add a prompt about who knows what when, especially for secrets, reveals, and unreliable viewpoints.
3. Update the offer sheet so it explicitly includes knowledge-state continuity alongside timeline, location, and relationship continuity.
4. Eventually create a second sample audit shaped around a campaign or adaptation workflow rather than a neat linear story.

## Conclusion

The continuity-audit idea is not just internally elegant. It appears sellable in at least one realistic messy-project scenario.

The next improvement is not a reinvention. It is sharper wording around secrets, knowledge, and campaign-shaped material.
