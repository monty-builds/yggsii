# Sample Continuity Audit, Campaign Adaptation Case

Project: Ashfall Ledger

Format: long-running tabletop campaign being reshaped into serial fiction

This sample exists to show the continuity-audit offer on messier source material than a neat linear outline.

The source is assumed to be ordered session recaps rather than polished chapters.

## Executive summary

The campaign spine is compelling, but the continuity risk is no longer basic chronology alone.

The main pressure points are knowledge-state drift, faction pressure decay, and one character disappearance that currently reads as accidental rather than loaded.

The story has enough structure to adapt cleanly, but it needs a pass that separates three things which tabletop play often tangles together:
- what actually happened
- what the party believes happened
- what the reader is allowed to know at each stage

If this were a real client project, the highest-value intervention would not be prose polish.

It would be a continuity pass focused on session order, reveal timing, and cast persistence.

## Project snapshot

### Material shape

- 18 ordered session recaps
- 6 recurring core characters
- 2 major home-base locations
- later travel arc with temporary stops
- rival faction pressure thread
- one hidden agenda character
- one failed mission remembered inconsistently by different characters

### Core continuity load

This project carries three continuity layers at once:
- travel and event order
- cast presence and absence
- knowledge and reveal logic

That makes it a good fit for a narrative debugging pass, because the likely failures are structural rather than sentence-level.

## What is working

### 1. The project has a recoverable spine

Even if the material is rough, ordered session recaps provide enough sequence to reconstruct the main arc.

That matters. A messy but ordered project is usually salvageable.

### 2. The cast appears to have distinct roles

A six-character recurring ensemble is large enough to create continuity risk, but also large enough to create real narrative value if handled cleanly.

This is not random sprawl. It is usable complexity.

### 3. The rival faction creates durable external pressure

A recurring outside force helps bind episodes together.

That thread can become a major continuity anchor if its influence is tracked consistently across the adaptation.

## Likely continuity watchpoints

### 1. Knowledge-state drift

Risk:
The adaptation may accidentally reveal information in the wrong order, especially if recap notes already blend player knowledge, character knowledge, and hindsight commentary.

What to check:
- who learns the hidden-agenda truth, and when
- whether any recap summary names motives before the fiction should make them legible
- whether the failed mission is narrated as mystery, rumour, or fact at each stage

Why it matters:
This is the sort of continuity break readers feel instantly even when they cannot name it.

### 2. Character disappearance without narrative framing

Risk:
A character who drops out for several sessions may read as accidental campaign absence unless the adaptation gives that absence meaning.

What to check:
- whether the disappearance is acknowledged in-world
- whether other characters adjust plans, trust, or emotional tone in response
- whether the return lands as payoff rather than bookkeeping

Why it matters:
In play, absences happen. In fiction, absences imply intention.

### 3. Travel timeline blur

Risk:
Once the story leaves its stable home-base locations, recap compression may flatten time, distance, and cause-effect.

What to check:
- whether each temporary stop has a clear before and after relation
- whether urgency claims match the implied travel time
- whether faction pressure plausibly follows the party across the travel arc

Why it matters:
Travel vagueness quietly destroys momentum if threat and movement stop feeling causal.

### 4. Rival faction pressure decay

Risk:
The rival faction may sound important in premise language but disappear between major confrontations.

What to check:
- whether the faction leaves evidence, consequences, or fear even in sessions where it is off-page
- whether every confrontation changes the party's picture of the faction in some way
- whether the adaptation overconcentrates the faction into a few dramatic beats and loses ambient pressure

Why it matters:
A strong antagonist force should distort the story continuously, not only during boss-fight scenes.

## Cast continuity notes

### Party ensemble

Likely strength:
The recurring cast is large enough for meaningful co-presence patterns and loyalty shifts.

Main watchpoint:
Scene and session summaries should track not only presence, but significance. A character can be physically present yet continuity-light if their motive or knowledge state is ignored.

### Hidden-agenda character

Likely strength:
This can provide excellent delayed-payoff structure.

Main watchpoint:
The adaptation must be ruthless about what the reader sees versus what later reinterpretation reveals.

### Missing character thread

Likely strength:
An accidental campaign absence can become a powerful narrative gap if reframed well.

Main watchpoint:
Do not let it sit as silent bookkeeping.

## Adaptation-specific risks

### 1. Recap language is not scene language

Recap notes often compress causality, skip emotional transitions, and summarise outcomes too early.

That means the audit should treat recap prose as evidence, not as ready-to-publish narrative structure.

### 2. Player logic is not always character logic

At the table, plans are often driven by player memory, table discussion, or improvisation.

In fiction, those same turns need in-world legibility.

### 3. Mystery handling gets harder in adaptation

A live game can survive fuzzy secrecy because the table co-creates the gaps.

A reader cannot. The adaptation must decide exactly when each piece of knowledge becomes visible.

## Highest-priority fixes if this were a real client project

### High priority

Build a reveal ledger.

Track who knows what, when, and how certain they are. This is the single best defence against knowledge-state continuity failure.

### High priority

Mark every session where the missing character is absent, referenced, suspected, or reintegrated.

That turns accidental table absence into controllable story structure.

### Medium priority

Create a travel spine before prose adaptation begins.

List each movement step, elapsed time, destination, and pressure consequence.

### Medium priority

Track rival faction pressure even in off-screen stretches.

If the faction matters, its effects should propagate beyond direct confrontation scenes.

## Audit conclusion

This kind of project is exactly why the continuity-audit offer exists.

The material is rich, but its main threats are not literary taste problems. They are structural truth problems.

A clean audit here would reduce rewrite debt by separating sequence, presence, and knowledge before prose adaptation hardens the wrong assumptions into canon.
